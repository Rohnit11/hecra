# Users Adopt AI Health Search Tools Based on Perceived Usefulness, Not Trust, and Integrate Them Into Multi-Platform "Mix-and-Match" Strategies

**Citation:** Wardle, C., Urbani, S., & Wang, E. (2025). "Evolving Health Information-Seeking Behavior in the Context of Google AI Overviews, ChatGPT, and Alexa: Interview Study Using the Think-Aloud Protocol." *Journal of Medical Internet Research*, 27, e79961.

**Paper:** 39 pages (PMC version). Published in JMIR, peer-reviewed. PMCID: PMC12541266.

---

## 1. Identification Strategy: Qualitative Descriptive (Not Causal)

This is a **qualitative descriptive study** using a **think-aloud protocol**. There is no causal identification strategy (no DiD, IV, RDD, or experiment with randomized assignment).

The authors conducted in-depth semi-structured interviews with 27 participants who searched for health information across 3 platforms -- Google (with AI Overviews), ChatGPT (primarily 3.5), and Amazon Alexa -- while verbalizing their thought processes in real time. Data were analyzed using **reflexive thematic analysis** (Braun & Clarke, 2006) through 6 phases: familiarization, individual coding, group code consolidation (34 codes), systematic application, theme construction, and collaborative review.

### Key methodological components:
- **Think-aloud protocol:** Participants narrated what they were seeing, clicking, and thinking during live searches (pp. 7-9)
- **4-phase study design:**
  - Phase 1: Self-reported search behavior (2 prompts, no actual searching)
  - Phase 2: Google search with narration
  - Phase 3: ChatGPT search with narration
  - Phase 4: Alexa search with narration
- **Two search prompts per participant:**
  - Prompt 1 (standardized): "Is St. John's wort effective at treating depression?" -- chosen as "unsettled science" topic
  - Prompt 2 (personal): Participant's own recent health question (Table 1, pp. 10-11)
- **Reflexive thematic analysis:** 3 researchers independently coded, then collaboratively identified 34 codes synthesized against 2 research questions (p. 14-15)
- **Ethics:** Brown University IRB approval (#00000404), May 20, 2024

### Research questions:
- RQ1: How have voice- and AI-assisted search technologies impacted HISBs?
- RQ2: How have these technologies impacted the ways in which people make sense of the health information they encounter?

## 3. Main Findings

**Note:** This is a qualitative study. There are no coefficient estimates, standard errors, or p-values. Findings are reported as thematic patterns with frequency counts where provided.

### 3.1 Platform-Specific Attitudes (pp. 15-17)

**Google AI Overviews:**
- ~1/3 of participants commented on AI Overviews during the interview
- **5 participants explicitly stated they skipped over AI Overviews** to review traditional search results
- **No participant reported stopping their search at AI Overviews** -- all continued scrolling
- Key concern: lack of sources

**Alexa:**
- **17/27 (63%)** said they would not typically use Alexa for health queries
- **8/27 (30%)** deemed voice-activated responses convenient only for low-stakes questions
- Broken links and misdirections to Amazon web pages led several participants to give up

**ChatGPT:**
- **14/27 (52%)** already used ChatGPT as part of their search routines
- **23/27 (85%)** reported favorably on some aspect of ChatGPT's formatting or efficiency (fast, organized, easy to digest)
- Valued for: summarizing, generating keywords for further searches, conversational follow-up capability
- **~2/3 of participants** explicitly expressed concern about ChatGPT not citing sources

### 3.2 Utility Over Trust: The Central Finding (pp. 17-18, 23)

- **15/27 (56%)** reported using AI-generated information despite expressing skepticism about trustworthiness, if it helped power further investigation
- Participants used ChatGPT as a **"starting point"** -- generating keywords, summaries, or angles to bring to Google or to a doctor's appointment
- Trust and usefulness did not always align: participants used tools they did not trust because of perceived utility
- This challenges existing HISB models that emphasize credibility as the primary driver of technology adoption

### 3.3 Factors Influencing Platform Choice (pp. 18-19)

Four contextual factors shaped platform selection:
1. **Severity of health concern:** More serious = more cross-platform verification
2. **Intended use of information:** Preparing for doctor visit vs. casual fact-check
3. **Circumstances:** Voice activation useful when driving or socializing; time-sensitivity mattered
4. **Prior knowledge/experience:** Familiarity with platform ownership (e.g., Amazon owning Alexa) shaped skepticism

### 3.4 Factors Increasing Likelihood of Using AI Content (pp. 19-20)

Three conditions made participants more likely to deem AI information useful:
1. **Familiarity/confirmation:** ~1/2 of participants trusted AI information more if it aligned with something previously seen or heard (illusory truth effect)
2. **Trustworthy sources identified:** Government websites, hospitals, academic institutions, studies
3. **Utility for subsequent searches:** Helped generate keywords or refine further investigation

### 3.5 Factors Decreasing Likelihood of Using AI Content (pp. 20-22)

Six conditions made participants less likely to deem AI information useful:
1. **No sourcing or low-quality sources** (most frequently cited issue across platforms)
2. **No human oversight:** ~2/3 expressed desire to know a human shaped the information
3. **Perceived built-in bias:** >1/3 concerned about company bias (Amazon/Alexa), political bias, or sycophancy (ChatGPT)
4. **Responses too brief** or too confident: **11/27 (41%)** found Alexa/AI Overviews too brief
5. **Voice activation issues:** >1/2 noted voice misunderstanding; multilingual users faced accent challenges
6. **Broken user experience:** **5/27** frustrated by broken "learn more" links on Alexa

### 3.6 Multilingual Experience (pp. 25)

- **9/27 (33%)** spoke English as second or third language
- Challenges included: voice recognition of accents, concern that non-English searches would yield geographically inappropriate results, cross-platform/cross-language search strategies
- LLM training data rooted in "Western medicine" may not satisfy users seeking indigenous or Eastern medicine practices

### 3.7 AI Literacy Gaps (p. 23)

- Only **5/27 (19%)** mentioned how "training data" could affect LLM results and impact credibility
- Misconceptions included: believing Google could be edited, believing ChatGPT just summarizes Google, confusion about company ownership

### 3.8 Echo Chamber Concerns (p. 24)

- Participants voiced concerns that LLMs reinforce existing beliefs (sycophancy)
- Familiarity-based trust intersects with illusory truth effect -- people trust repeated information even if false
- Authors note recent studies confirm LLM sycophancy concerns are warranted

## 4. Limitations

- **Small sample size (N=27)** generated via convenience sampling through personal networks -- cannot generalize to wider population (p. 25-26)
- **Hawthorne effect:** Think-aloud protocol may change behavior because participants know they are being observed (p. 26)
- **Not longitudinal:** Snapshot at one point in time (summer-fall 2024) (p. 26)
- **Technology changed during study:** Google AI Overviews expanded and got longer; both Google Overviews and ChatGPT now link to sources -- a majority of criticisms about absent sources would likely differ today (p. 26)
- **Oversampling bias:** Deliberately oversampled younger, university-educated participants (74% had at least undergraduate degree; 74% under 45) (p. 14)
- **37% first-time LLM users:** 10/27 had never used ChatGPT before, so observations reflect first impressions rather than established behavior (p. 26)
- **Geographic limitation:** All participants from US east coast (4 locations in NJ/NY/RI) (p. 11)
- **No screen recording:** Participants' screen activity was not recorded; relied on verbal narration (p. 11)

## 5. Key Implications

1. **Utility can outweigh trust in HISB:** Existing models emphasizing credibility as the primary driver of health information adoption may need revision -- perceived usefulness and convenience can drive use even when trust is low
2. **AI tools as "starting points," not replacements:** Users integrate AI into multi-platform strategies rather than treating it as a standalone source; ChatGPT serves as a keyword generator and summarizer that feeds into deeper Google searches
3. **Digital health literacy needs updating:** Only 19% of participants understood how training data affects LLM outputs -- cross-generational educational programs are needed
4. **Echo chamber risks:** The combination of LLM sycophancy and the illusory truth effect (trusting repeated information) creates potential for reinforcing health misinformation
5. **Multilingual and cultural challenges:** Voice recognition failures, Western medicine bias in training data, and cross-language search strategies create inequitable experiences for non-English speakers
6. **Source transparency matters:** The absence of citations was the most frequently cited concern across all platforms -- now partially addressed by design changes in Google Overviews and ChatGPT
7. **Existing HISB models remain relevant:** Freimuth et al.'s (1989) 6-step model appeared in all 27 conversations, but AI tools introduce simultaneous exploration-evaluation dynamics rather than sequential steps
8. **Methodological recommendation:** Researchers should design longitudinal mixed-methods studies and allow participants to explore personally relevant health questions, not just standardized prompts
